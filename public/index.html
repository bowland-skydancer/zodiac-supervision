About This App
What This Is, and What It Isn't
This is a structured reflective thinking aid for practising coaches who already have human supervision. It extends the supervisory space between sessions. It is not supervision.
What it does

The Supervision Council uses twelve animal perspectives, each representing a distinct supervisory viewpoint, to help coaches think more broadly about their practice. In group mode, each participant holds one animal and generates questions from it. In self-supervision mode, an AI reads your case and selects the most relevant perspectives, tailoring the questions to your specific situation. In supervision prep mode, the same perspectives help you articulate what you are bringing to a human supervision session and arrive ready.

The design deliberately resists resolution. The perspectives generate questions, not answers. The aim is to widen inquiry, not close it down.

Honest limitations

No relational presence. Supervision works through the quality of a real relationship: presence, attunement, the supervisor's genuine response to what they're hearing. This app can simulate curiosity but not experience it. It has no stake in the relationship, no felt sense, no counter-transference to notice and work with.

No parallel process. One of supervision's most powerful phenomena, where the dynamics between coach and client are unconsciously re-enacted in the supervision relationship, cannot occur here. An AI cannot be recruited into a parallel process, and therefore cannot name one.

No professional accountability. When a case involves safeguarding concerns, risk of harm, or complex ethical edges, supervision requires judgment grounded in professional responsibility. This app has no registration, no duty of care in any legally meaningful sense. It can surface a question; it cannot hold the weight of the responsibility.

The risk of false closure. Well-formed questions and tidy frameworks can create an illusion of having been supervised. This app includes a specific prompt designed to counteract this, but the risk remains. If the process feels complete, treat that as data, not as a signal to stop.

On the animal perspectives. The twelve perspectives used in this app were originally inspired by the animals of the Chinese Zodiac, reimagined and substantially redeveloped for coaching supervision. The zodiac connection has been set aside; what remains is a set of twelve distinct supervisory lenses, each held by an animal character chosen for how well it represents that perspective.

What it can honestly offer

Available between sessions. Human supervision is time-bounded. This app is available whenever a case is pressing: at 11pm before a difficult session, or immediately after one.

Non-judgmental space. Some coaches find it easier to surface things they'd feel exposed bringing to a human supervisor, at least initially. This app can serve as a thinking-through space before a human conversation.

Multiple perspectives simultaneously. The animal structure deliberately brings competing angles to bear on a single case, reducing the risk of tunnel vision.

Widening access. Regular human supervision is expensive and not always accessible. Used honestly, this app can support coaches who would otherwise have no reflective space at all.

A note on confidentiality

In self-supervision and supervision prep modes, the text you write is sent to an AI language model for processing. Before submitting, please anonymise all client details: remove names, identifying roles, organisations, and any information that could identify the person you are writing about. Your clients have not consented to their situation being processed by an AI system.

When to use human supervision instead

If your case involves a safeguarding concern, a risk of harm to any person, a serious ethical dilemma, or significant distress in yourself as a coach, please bring it to a qualified human supervisor. This app will signal that too, but you don't need the app to tell you: if the stakes feel high, take it to a person.

The Supervision Council
Explore the Animals
Twelve perspectives on coaching supervision, each held by a distinct animal. Together they form a complete supervisory landscape.
Formative
Supervision that supports the coach's learning and professional development. These animals notice where growth is possible, where edges are being avoided, and where practice could deepen.
🦅
Eagle
Vision, Ambition & Transformation
Eagle sees from altitude. Where other animals are close to the ground, Eagle holds the long view, watching for what the client could become, what the coach might be underselling, and where genuine transformation is possible rather than incremental adjustment. Eagle asks the boldest questions and resists the pull toward the merely comfortable or achievable. In supervision, Eagle notices when the work has become too small for the person doing it.
🐅
Tiger
Challenge, Stretch & Avoidance
Tiger notices what is not being said and where comfort has become collusion. This is the animal that names the unspoken, identifies where coach or client is playing safe, and asks what risk needs to be taken next. Tiger is not aggressive; it is precise. It does not challenge for the sake of it but because something important is being carefully avoided. In supervision, Tiger asks the question that creates a slight resistance in the room.
🐒
Monkey
Experiment, Intervention & Craft
Monkey is interested in what the coach actually does in the room. Not the theory, not the intention, but the specific interventions, questions, and moves that either landed or did not. Monkey is playful, curious, and practically minded. It asks what could be tried differently, what small experiment would stretch the practice, and what the coach is defaulting to without noticing. In supervision, Monkey keeps the coach honest about their craft.
Normative
Supervision that attends to professional standards, ethical responsibility, and the boundaries of the coaching role. These animals notice where the frame needs holding, where duty is at stake, and where clarity is being avoided.
🦢
Swan
Contract, Frame & Ethics
Swan is precise, disciplined, and unhurried. This animal holds the coaching frame with quiet authority, noticing where boundaries have blurred, where the contract needs revisiting, and where ethical edges are present. Swan does not raise alarms unnecessarily but it does not look away either. In supervision, Swan asks whether what is happening is still coaching, and whether the work is being conducted with appropriate rigour and care.
🦉
Owl
Clarity, Precision & Truth-Telling
Owl sees in the dark. It notices vagueness, polite indirection, and the things being named obliquely or not at all. Where other animals might soften the edges, Owl asks for precision. It holds the coach to account for what they are observing but not saying, what they know but are not naming. In supervision, Owl asks what the most accurate description of this situation actually is, and what is preventing the coach from saying it plainly.
🐕
Dog
Loyalty, Duty & Allegiances
Dog is deeply loyal, and that loyalty is exactly what this animal examines. Whose interests is the coach most loyal to: the client, the sponsor, the organisation, their own professional comfort? Dog notices where protective instincts are operating, where duty is guiding well and where it is constraining, and where fidelity to the client might be tipping into collusion or over-protection. Dog also attends to questions of power, difference, and identity: whose perspective is centred, whose is marginalised, and what cultural context shapes this coaching relationship. In supervision, Dog asks who is really being served by the choices the coach is making.
Restorative
Supervision that attends to the coach's inner experience, wellbeing, and the relational quality of the work. These animals notice what is being carried, what is tender, and what the coach needs in order to continue working well.
🐇
Rabbit
Relationship, Safety & Alliance
Rabbit is attuned to the emotional climate and the pace of the work. It notices where trust is fragile, where someone feels exposed or defended, and whether the coaching relationship has the safety it needs to do meaningful work. Rabbit does not avoid difficulty but insists that difficulty is held within a relationship that can bear it. In supervision, Rabbit asks what the coaching relationship needs right now, and whether the coach is moving at the right speed.
🦌
Deer
Vulnerability, Care & the Unspoken Need
Deer moves softly and senses what others miss. This animal attends to what the client has not yet been able to ask for, what is being carried quietly in the work, and whether the coach's care for the person is clear-eyed or softening what needs to be seen. Deer also holds the somatic and embodied dimension: what the coach feels in the room, in their body, before language catches up. In supervision, Deer asks what genuine tenderness, not sentimentality, looks like in relation to this client.
🐻
Bear
Meaning, Nourishment & Sufficiency
Bear knows how to rest, how to replenish, and what truly sustains. This animal asks what meaning the client and coach are each making of the work, whether there is a sense of sufficiency and abundance or something important feeling scarce, and what is genuinely nourishing about the coaching relationship. Bear notices depletion quietly but persistently. In supervision, Bear asks whether the work is feeding the people doing it, and whether what matters most is receiving the attention it deserves.
Generative
Supervision that attends to patterns across practice, sustainability over time, and the coach's capacity to adapt and renew. These animals notice what is recurring, what is stagnating, and what resources are being drawn on or depleted.
🦫
Beaver
Resources, Sustainability & Adaptive Capacity
Beaver is purposeful and resourceful. It notices what the coach and client are actually drawing on, where over-reliance on a single approach is limiting the work, and where depletion is showing up. Beaver is not merely practical; it builds something that holds. In supervision, Beaver asks what adaptive moves have not yet been tried, and whether the coach is working sustainably or running on reserves they may not have noticed are running low.
🐺
Wolf
Sense-making, Pattern & Timing
Wolf reads the system. It notices what keeps recurring, what the dynamics in a case echo from other cases, and what the coach's own responses might be telling them about a deeper pattern. Wolf is patient and attuned, waiting for the right moment before acting. It also holds the parallel process dimension: the way dynamics between coach and client can unconsciously be re-enacted in the supervision relationship itself. In supervision, Wolf asks what pattern is present here that has been present before, and what the timing of this realisation means.
🐎
Horse
Direction, Energy & Movement
Horse notices when the coaching has lost its momentum or its direction. It asks whether the work is actually moving the client, where drift or stagnation has set in, and whether the coach is colluding with a comfortable lack of progress. Horse is not impatient; it is oriented. It wants to know where the work is going and whether the energy in the room is generative or spent. In supervision, Horse asks what shift in direction or pace is needed, and whether the coach has noticed they have stopped moving.
